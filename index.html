Quantum Computing
Fundamentals

Three weird properties that make quantum computers fundamentally different — explained with a coin, two dice, and waves.

🪙 Property 1 — Superposition

A classical coin is either Heads (0) or Tails (1). You know exactly which one. A quantum coin — while in flight — is both at once. It only "picks" a side when you look at it (measure it). This is what a qubit does. The qubit lives in superposition: α|0⟩ + β|1⟩.

The quantum insight: Before measurement, the qubit holds both possibilities simultaneously. A 3-qubit system can be in 8 states at once; 300 qubits hold 2³⁰⁰ states — more than atoms in the universe. That's quantum parallelism.

🎲 Property 2 — Entanglement

Imagine two magical dice. When entangled, rolling one instantly determines the other — no matter how far apart they are. The two are correlated beyond classical physics. In quantum computing, entangled qubits share a joint state that cannot be described independently. Einstein called it "spooky action at a distance" — but it's real and experimentally verified.

🌊 Property 3 — Interference

Quantum states are waves, not just particles. When two quantum paths overlap, they can constructively interfere (amplify correct answers) or destructively interfere (cancel wrong answers). Quantum algorithms are choreographed interference patterns — they boost the probability of the right answer and kill off the wrong ones.

Wave A — Quantum path 1 (probability amplitude)
Wave B — Quantum path 2
Result — Combined wave (what you measure)
Why this matters for algorithms: Grover's search and Shor's factoring algorithm both work by engineering interference — wrong answer paths destructively cancel, correct answer paths constructively amplify. This is the engine behind quantum speedup.

🔮 What is Quantum Machine Learning?

Before we go deeper in the next module, here's the short version — where QML actually sits at the intersection of these fields.

Quantum Machine Learning (QML) is an interdisciplinary field that merges quantum computing with traditional AI. By using qubits, superposition, and entanglement, QML algorithms aim to process massive datasets, accelerate training times, and solve computationally difficult tasks exponentially faster than classical machine learning.

Core Concepts

Quantum-enhanced ML
Quantum algorithms for classical data

Uses quantum processors (QPUs) to execute specific tasks — like linear algebra, matrix inversion, or optimization problems — speeding up the classical machine learning pipeline.

ML for Quantum
Machine learning for quantum systems

Utilizes classical AI techniques to help researchers understand quantum phenomena, calibrate quantum hardware, and discover new quantum error-correcting codes.

FAQ

Fundamentally, quantum AI builds upon the ideas of quantum computing and artificial intelligence to provide a system that is more robust and effective. Information is processed by conventional computers using bits, which can be either a 0 or a 1 — quantum AI instead leans on qubits and quantum effects to process information.
QML is a technique of combining machine learning and quantum computing concepts, in which qubits are used instead of bits and the model is trained. QML can aid in the speeding up of existing algorithms, the development of new algorithms, and the solution of more complex problems.
Quantum machine learning employs the power of quantum computers to provide faster, more accurate computations as well as increased scalability. Because of the unique quantum properties, multiple solutions can be explored at the same time, resulting in more efficient problem-solving. Furthermore, quantum algorithms may reveal insights in data analysis that classical methods cannot.
QML offers immense potential in solving data processing, pattern recognition, and optimization problems. In situations where classical methods have computational difficulties, quantum algorithms might perform better.
02 Module Two · 12–28 min

Quantum Machine
Learning

Classical ML hits walls. QML proposes a new compute paradigm for AI.

What is QML? For this module we focus on one concrete, well-understood corner of QML: quantum kernel methods and the Quantum Support Vector Machine. The core idea — encode data into a quantum state, then use the geometry of that quantum state space to separate classes — is one of the most rigorously studied paths to a provable quantum advantage in machine learning.
🔑

Quantum Kernel Methods

Maps classical data into an exponentially large Hilbert space via a quantum feature map circuit. The "kernel" — a similarity score between two data points — is computed as the overlap between their quantum states, on quantum hardware.

🧮

Quantum SVM

A classical Support Vector Machine where the kernel matrix is supplied by a quantum computer instead of a classical formula. The optimization (finding the max-margin separator) still runs classically — only the kernel estimation is quantum.

Why Quantum Kernels? The Relevance for AI

n qubits define a 2ⁿ-dimensional Hilbert space. A quantum feature map can embed classical data into this space, meaning the "lift" performed by a quantum kernel can access feature spaces far larger than any classical kernel could represent explicitly — though whether that translates into a usable advantage depends heavily on the dataset.
Classically, the kernel trick lets an SVM operate in a high-dimensional feature space without ever computing the coordinates explicitly — only the inner products K(x,x') = φ(x)·φ(x') are needed. A quantum kernel does the same thing, but φ(x) is the quantum state produced by a feature-map circuit, and the inner product is estimated by measuring state overlap.
For most natural datasets, classical kernels (RBF, polynomial) work just as well or better. The clearest theoretical separations come from datasets specifically constructed around problems believed to be classically hard, such as discrete logarithm-based classification tasks — demonstrating that a quantum advantage is possible but not automatic.
K(x,x') = |⟨Φ(x)|Φ(x')⟩|² is estimated by running a feature-map circuit (e.g. a ZZFeatureMap) for x, its inverse for x', and measuring the probability of returning to the all-zero state — or via a swap test between two copies of the state. Each entry of the kernel matrix needs many circuit shots to estimate accurately.

Quantum SVM — Complexity at a Glance

TaskClassical ComplexityQuantum ComplexityNotes
Kernel matrix entry (per pair)O(d) — direct formulaO(circuit depth) + shotsd = feature dimension; quantum cost dominated by shot count for accurate estimation
Quantum SVM (engineered kernel)O(N·d)O(log(N·d)) *Theoretical speedup shown only for specially constructed, classically-hard datasets

* Holds for datasets engineered to be classically hard (e.g. based on discrete-log problems); not a general-purpose speedup over classical SVM on typical real-world data.

Current Challenges: Quantum kernel values can suffer from exponential concentration — as qubit count or circuit depth grows, kernel entries collapse toward a constant value, making classes indistinguishable and the kernel matrix unlearnable. Add shot noise from finite measurements, and the fact that many "quantum" kernels turn out to be classically simulable, and the practical case for quantum kernels remains narrow and dataset-specific (see Huang et al., "Power of data in quantum machine learning," 2021).

03 Module Three · 28–48 min

Post-Quantum
Cryptography

Quantum computers will break RSA and ECC. NIST has a plan. Lattices are the answer.

⚠️ The Threat: Shor's algorithm running on a sufficiently powerful quantum computer can break RSA-2048 in hours. The same goes for Elliptic Curve Cryptography (ECC). All internet traffic secured by HTTPS today could be retroactively decrypted — a threat called "harvest now, decrypt later."

Timeline: Cryptography & Quantum Threat

1977
RSA Algorithm Published
Rivest-Shamir-Adleman — security based on difficulty of integer factorization. Becomes backbone of internet security for decades.
1994
Shor's Algorithm
Peter Shor proves quantum computers can factor integers in polynomial time — RSA's theoretical death warrant is signed.
2016
NIST Begins PQC Standardization
National Institute of Standards and Technology launches a global competition for post-quantum cryptographic algorithms. 69 submissions received.
2022
NIST Selects Final Candidates
CRYSTALS-Kyber, CRYSTALS-Dilithium, FALCON, SPHINCS+ selected for standardization. Lattice-based algorithms dominate.
2024
NIST PQC Standards Finalized
FIPS 203 (ML-KEM / Kyber), FIPS 204 (ML-DSA / Dilithium), FIPS 205 (SLH-DSA / SPHINCS+) officially published. Transition begins.

🔷 Lattice Cryptography — Visualized

A lattice is a regular grid of points in high-dimensional space. The hard problem: given a "noisy" target point, find the closest lattice point — this is the Learning With Errors (LWE) problem, believed to resist quantum attack. Drag to rotate and scroll to zoom on an actual 3D lattice grid below.

3d lattice by dansmath on Sketchfab

Each ball marks an integer lattice point in 3D coordinate space — in real LWE-based schemes like Kyber, this grid lives in 500+ dimensions, which is exactly what makes the closest-point problem intractable even for a quantum computer.

Post-Quantum Hard Problems

🔷

LWE — Learning With Errors

Given A and b = As + e (mod q) where e is small random noise, find secret vector s. Reducing this to lattice problems makes it quantum-resistant. Basis of Kyber (ML-KEM).

b = As + e (mod q)
🔢

RLWE — Ring-LWE

LWE over polynomial rings — far more efficient with smaller key sizes. Ring structure allows use of NTT (Number Theoretic Transform) for fast arithmetic. Powers Dilithium & Kyber.

🌲

Hash-Based Signatures

SPHINCS+ uses only hash functions (SHA-3 / SHAKE-256) — security relies solely on hash collision resistance, well-understood for decades. Larger signatures but maximum trust.

🔐

Code-Based Cryptography

McEliece (1978!) based on decoding random linear codes. NIST alternate candidate HQC uses this. Huge key sizes historically, but quantum-resistant since the 70s.

Why Lattices Win: Lattice problems (SVP, CVP, LWE) have no known quantum speedup beyond √2 (Grover-like). Even a 10,000-qubit fault-tolerant quantum computer cannot break CRYSTALS-Kyber with 256-bit security. This makes lattice crypto the most future-proof option known today.

Bonus: Quantum Key Distribution (QKD)

📡

BB84 Protocol

Alice sends qubits in random polarizations. Any eavesdropping by Eve disturbs the quantum states (no-cloning theorem), revealing her presence. Provably secure by physics, not math.

🛰️

QKD vs PQC

QKD is hardware-dependent (fiber/satellite, expensive, short range) vs PQC which is pure software. For most applications, PQC (NIST standards) is the practical path forward.

Learning Platforms

You don't need a quantum computer to learn quantum computing. Here's where to start.

IBM Quantum
Cloud · Real Hardware + Simulator

Free access to real quantum processors (127+ qubit Eagle, 133-qubit Heron). Qiskit SDK in Python. Composer GUI for circuit drag-and-drop. Best for getting hands-on quickly.

quantum.ibm.com Free Tier
Qiskit
Open Source SDK · Python

IBM's open-source quantum SDK. Qiskit Terra (circuits), Qiskit Aer (simulation), Qiskit Nature (chemistry), Qiskit ML (quantum ML primitives). Largest community ecosystem.

qiskit.org Open Source
PennyLane
QML Framework · Xanadu

Best framework for Quantum Machine Learning. Automatic differentiation through quantum circuits, integrates with PyTorch and TensorFlow. JAX backend available. QML-first design.

pennylane.ai Open Source
Google Cirq
Framework · Google Research

Google's quantum circuit library. Designed for NISQ algorithms. Powers Sycamore experiments. TFQ (TensorFlow Quantum) built on top for hybrid ML. Great docs and research focus.

quantumai.google Open Source
AWS Braket
Cloud · Multi-Hardware

Access IonQ (trapped ion), Rigetti (superconducting), Oxford Quantum Circuits hardware, plus managed simulators. Pay-per-use. Great for research teams comparing hardware paradigms.

aws.amazon.com/braket Paid
Quirk
Browser Simulator · No Install

Drag-and-drop quantum circuit simulator that runs entirely in the browser. No account, no install. Perfect for classroom demonstrations and intuition building. Real-time state visualization.

algassert.com/quirk Free · No Login
Quantum Katas
Learning · Microsoft Q#

Microsoft's self-paced quantum programming tutorials using Q# language. Jupyter notebooks covering superposition, measurements, quantum oracles, Grover's algorithm. Excellent pedagogy.

github.com/microsoft/QuantumKatas
IBMQ Learning
Course Platform

Free structured courses: "Introduction to Quantum Computing," "Understanding Quantum Information," "Quantum Machine Learning." Certificates available. Badges through Credly.
